The core idea

Two security jobs. Never conflate them.

Most bridges get robbed because they blur these. pBTC keeps them separate and is explicit about where trust remains.

Job 1 — Verification

Did the deposit really happen?

A question of fact, answered by math: an on-chain Bitcoin light client checks proof-of-work and Merkle inclusion. Minting requires a full SPV proof — no optimistic shortcut at launch.

→ trust-minimized by mathematics
Job 2 — Custody

Who holds the BTC keys?

The honeypot, and the hard problem — roughly half of all bridge losses are custody-key compromise. Solved by distribution and slashing, not by verification. Target: a 51-of-100 threshold signing group.

→ trust assumption during interim phases, labeled as such

Deposit flow

How a BTC deposit becomes pBTC

Five steps, each verifiable. Redemption runs the same path in reverse.

01

Derive

Get a unique BTC deposit address from your PulseChain address + the wallet key.

02

Send & reveal

Send BTC to it and reveal the deposit on PulseChain.

03

Sweep

The signing group sweeps the deposit into the bridge wallet on Bitcoin.

04

Prove

An SPV proof of the sweep is checked on-chain against verified difficulty.

05

Mint

pBTC is minted 1:1 against the proven deposit.

Architecture

Three layers, each doing one job

Layer 1 · Math

On-chain SPV

A Bitcoin light client (LightRelay) verifies headers and inclusion. This makes BTC → PulseChain trust-minimized without relying on anyone's honesty.

Layer 2 · Keys

Distributed custody

A threshold-ECDSA signing group holds the BTC — no single person, host, or cloud — with staking and slashing. The hard problem, phased in honestly.

Layer 3 · Watch

Validator guardians

Validators run sidecars that relay headers, watchtower every action, and can veto fraud and pause — bonded and slashable. Breadth genuinely helps here.
